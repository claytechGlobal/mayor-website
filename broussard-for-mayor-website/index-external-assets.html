To the Citizens of Opelousas

Today, I stand before you with a deep sense of responsibility and commitment to officially announce my candidacy for Mayor of the City of Opelousas.

Opelousas is more than just where we live — it is our home, our history, and our future. For too long, many in our community have felt unheard, underserved, and overlooked. My campaign is built on one promise: Building with Broussard — From the Ground Up. Vision alone is not enough — our city needs specific, measurable action.

My Commitments to Opelousas

Accountability & Transparency

Implement quarterly public financial reports, require independent audits of major departments, and establish a citizen oversight committee to ensure tax dollars are spent responsibly. City contracts and expenditures will be accessible to the public.

Economic Growth & Job Creation

Create a targeted business recruitment plan focused on light industry, logistics, and small manufacturing. Establish an “Open for Business” fast-track permitting system, reduce unnecessary red tape, and partner with regional and state agencies to bring investment into Opelousas. Youth & Workforce Development

Introduce clear ethics policies for city officials, including conflict-of-interest standards and mandatory disclosures, to restore trust in local government.

Water & Sewer Modernization

The City of Opelousas will undertake a full-scale water and sewer infrastructure overhaul across approximately 9.8 square miles, modernizing critical systems that have been neglected for decades. This project will be financed through the U.S. EPA’s WIFIA program, allowing us to rebuild our infrastructure without placing the burden upfront on local taxpayers.

What This Project Will Do

We will replace and upgrade the core systems that every home, business, and neighborhood depends on:

  • Replace aging water lines to stop leaks and improve pressure
  • Rebuild sewer systems to eliminate overflows and backups
  • Repair and restore manholes to reduce infiltration and system strain
  • Upgrade pump stations for reliability and efficiency
  • Install smart meters and leak-detection technology for accountability and savings

Result: a safer, cleaner, more reliable water system for every resident and business in the City of Opelousas.

How We Will Pay for It

This is a smart financing strategy, not a tax-heavy plan. Our guiding principle: use federal dollars first, and protect local taxpayers.

Estimated funding sources
Funding SourceShare
Federal WIFIA funding (low-interest, long-term)up to 49%
State infrastructure funds20–30%
Local matching funds and bonds10–20%
Available grants5–15%

A Phased, Accountable Approach

Phase 1 · Years 1–3

Stop the worst failures

Fix the worst water-line failures, eliminate major sewer overflow areas, and launch smart-system upgrades.

Phase 2 · Years 3–6

Expand & modernize

Expand full neighborhood replacements and modernize pump stations.

Phase 3 · Years 6–10

Optimize & future-proof

Optimize system performance and build long-term reliability and redundancy.

Why This Matters

This is more than pipes and infrastructure — this is about the future of Opelousas. This plan will:

  • Eliminate boil-water advisories
  • Reduce waste and water loss
  • Improve public health and environmental safety
  • Support business growth and job creation
  • Lower long-term maintenance and emergency repair costs

Execution Plan

To move forward immediately, we will:

  • Commission a Preliminary Engineering Report (PER)
  • Conduct a citywide infrastructure assessment
  • Develop a 10-year financial plan
  • Engage financial and engineering experts
  • Submit a WIFIA Letter of Interest

We cannot grow Opelousas on failing infrastructure.

This plan delivers real solutions, measurable results, and a stronger, more reliable city for everyone — a generational rebuild, done right, with smart funding instead of a tax burden.

Roads & Bridges Modernization

The City of Opelousas will seek federal investment through the U.S. DOT BUILD Grant Program to modernize critical transportation infrastructure citywide — the Opelousas Roads & Bridges Modernization Initiative (ORBMI). This project will reconstruct deteriorating roadways, rehabilitate aging bridges, improve drainage and flood resilience, modernize traffic systems, strengthen freight connectivity, and support long-term economic growth.

Project Need

Opelousas faces major transportation challenges across four areas:

  • Aging road network — deferred maintenance, chronic potholes, base failures, and poor pavement ratings
  • Aging bridges & drainage structures — culvert failures, structural wear, and flood vulnerability

  • Economic bottlenecks — slowed freight movement, harder industrial recruitment, and stalled downtown redevelopment

Drainage & Resiliency

Flood mitigation through stormwater upgrades, roadside drainage, and detention enhancements.

Smart Traffic Improvements

Adaptive traffic signals, smart school-zone systems, emergency preemption, and traffic analytics.

Engineering, Design & Construction Management

Professional design and oversight to deliver the program on time and on budget.

Stormwater Master Plan

The City of Opelousas requires a comprehensive Stormwater Master Plan to address recurring neighborhood flooding, aging drainage infrastructure, increasing rainfall intensity, regulatory compliance, and long-term climate resilience. This plan creates a citywide drainage roadmap to modernize stormwater systems, reduce flood risk, protect roads and homes, and support future development. Planning horizon: 2026–2040.

Core Objectives

  • Flood reduction — reduce localized street and neighborhood flooding
  • Infrastructure modernization — upgrade undersized or failing drainage assets
  • Water-quality improvement — reduce runoff pollutants entering local waterways
  • Climate resilience — prepare for stronger storm events and heavier rainfall
  • Economic protection — protect homes, businesses, and public assets

Existing Challenges

  • Undersized culverts and clogged roadside ditches
  • Failing storm drains and limited detention capacity
  • Repetitive flood-loss areas and roadway ponding
  • Erosion around channels and outfalls

Master Plan Components

Citywide Drainage Asset Inventory

Map all storm drains, inlets, pipes, canals, culverts, detention facilities, and outfalls. Deliverable: a GIS-based digital stormwater map.

Hydrologic & Hydraulic Modeling

Use rainfall/runoff modeling, floodplain analysis, and watershed mapping to identify flood hotspots, capacity bottlenecks, and priority projects.

Capital Improvement Program

Upsize undersized storm lines, replace aging culverts, desilt and widen canals, and add detention/retention — ponds, underground storage, and neighborhood basins.

Green Infrastructure

Nature-based solutions — bioswales, rain gardens, permeable pavement, tree-canopy expansion, and green streets — lowering runoff and reducing heat.

Smart Stormwater System

Real-time flood sensors, pump telemetry, rain gauges, and a digital dashboard — a Smart Stormwater Command Center.

One Safe Opelousas 2026–2031

ONE SAFE OPELOUSAS is a citywide public safety strategy designed to reduce violent crime, strengthen neighborhoods, improve trust in public institutions, and create safer opportunities for youth and families. It combines targeted law enforcement, community intervention, youth prevention, technology modernization, neighborhood revitalization, and accountability. Public safety is not just policing — it is prevention, intervention, and partnership. Initiative horizon: 2026–2031.

Core Pillars

Violent Crime Reduction

Focus on gun violence, repeat offenders, group violence, and domestic-violence hotspots through data-driven patrol deployment, focused deterrence, hot-spot policing, and coordinated prosecution support.

Goal: reduce violent crime 25% in 5 years

Community Policing & Trust

Expand neighborhood patrol visibility, hold monthly public-safety forums, and build block-captain and faith-based partnerships through “Know Your Officer” and Neighborhood Safety Councils.

Youth Intervention & Diversion

After-school safe spaces, mentoring, a summer-jobs pipeline, violence-interruption teams, and school-based prevention — in partnership with the St. Landry Parish School Board, churches, and nonprofits.

Technology Modernization

A public-safety camera network, policy-controlled license-plate readers, a real-time crime center, smart street lighting, and an anonymous tip app — the Opelousas Public Safety Operations Center.

Blight & Environmental Safety

Target abandoned properties, illegal dumping, dark corridors, and nuisance properties. Cleaner, brighter neighborhoods reduce crime. Program: Clean & Safe Blocks.

Reentry & Second Chance

Workforce placement, record-clearing clinics, housing support, and behavioral-support referrals to reduce recidivism.

Public-Private Partnership Initiative

A Public–Private Partnership (PPP) Initiative would allow the City of Opelousas to attract private investment, accelerate infrastructure improvements, and create new revenue-generating assets without placing the full financial burden on taxpayers. PPPs are increasingly used across Louisiana to deliver infrastructure and economic-development projects by sharing costs, risks, and expertise between government and private partners.

Transform Opelousas into a regional hub for business, innovation, housing, and infrastructure through strategic partnerships between government and the private sector.

Priority PPP Projects

Infrastructure Investment Program

Private firms design, build, finance, and maintain portions of water, sewer, smart stormwater, road, bridge, and traffic systems under performance contracts — while the City retains ownership and sets standards. Faster delivery, lower upfront cost, and long-term maintenance guarantees.

Downtown Redevelopment Partnership

Renovate historic buildings, create mixed-use developments, expand housing, and improve streetscapes — with local developers, preservation investors, CDFIs, and Opportunity Zone investors. Revenue from property, sales, lease, and parking.

Technology & Broadband Partnership

Citywide fiber, smart-city sensors, public Wi-Fi, and traffic and utility monitoring — attracting remote workers, supporting business growth, and improving public safety.

Industrial & Logistics Park

Develop a shovel-ready industrial park along key corridors. The City contributes land, access, and incentives; the private partner handles site prep, construction, and tenant recruitment. Aligns with Louisiana’s FastSites program.

Affordable Housing Partnership

Workforce, senior, and mixed-income housing financed through federal tax credits, private equity, state programs, and Opportunity Zone investment — increasing supply and revitalizing neighborhoods.

Sports, Entertainment & Events District

A multi-use sports complex, festival grounds, conference center, and entertainment venue. The private partner builds and operates; the City contributes land and infrastructure. Revenue from events, sponsorships, hotel taxes, and leases.

An Independent PPP Authority

Create an independent Opelousas Public-Private Partnership Authority responsible for soliciting private investment, evaluating proposals, negotiating agreements, managing performance, and ensuring transparency. Its board could include the Mayor, City Council, business and financial leaders, economic-development representatives, and community stakeholders.

Jobs & Growth — OEDA

The Opelousas Economic Development Administration (OEDA) would serve as the city’s lead agency for attracting investment, creating jobs, supporting local businesses, and coordinating public-private partnerships. Its mission: create jobs, expand the tax base, revitalize neighborhoods, and position Opelousas as a regional hub for business, manufacturing, logistics, technology, healthcare, and tourism.

Core Objectives

Business Attraction

Recruit new manufacturers and industrial employers, market Opelousas nationally and internationally, develop shovel-ready sites, and coordinate incentive packages.

Business Retention & Expansion

Visit existing businesses regularly, assist with workforce development, help businesses expand locally, and remove barriers to growth.

Small Business Development

A one-stop assistance center, entrepreneur training, grants and revolving loans, and minority- and women-owned business initiatives.

Workforce Development

Partner with schools, colleges, and technical institutions on apprenticeships, skilled-trades training, and career placement.

Downtown Revitalization

Façade-improvement grants, historic-preservation incentives, restaurant and retail recruitment, and mixed-use redevelopment.

Industrial Development

Industrial-park expansion, logistics and distribution recruitment, rail and highway development, and utility-infrastructure improvements.

Tourism & Cultural Economy

Heritage tourism, festivals and events, arts and entertainment districts, and sports-tourism initiatives.

Organizational Structure

An Executive Director would lead the agency, reporting to the Mayor and to the City Council through annual reporting, organized into five divisions:

  • Business Recruitment — attraction, site selection, and corporate relations
  • Small Business & Entrepreneurship — incubator, technical assistance, and access to capital
  • Planning & Redevelopment — downtown projects, property redevelopment, and Opportunity Zones
  • Workforce Development — industry partnerships, job training, and employment initiatives
  • Grants & Public-Private Partnerships — federal and state grants, private investment, and infrastructure financing
